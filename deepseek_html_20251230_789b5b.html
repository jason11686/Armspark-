Introduction

Armspark introduces a revolutionary multiple bilateral upper limb rehabilitation training equipment designed to address the critical needs of patients with neurological conditions, such as stroke, and those with limb loss, who face significant upper limb dysfunction. Unlike conventional large, complex, and unilateral rehabilitation devices, this equipment offers a compact, user-friendly solution that can be used at home, enhancing accessibility and independence.

The Armspark system features two specialized modes—circular and rectangular motion paths—and an individualized mode allowing free limb movement while controlling a mini-game character, promoting engagement and reducing discomfort during recovery. Its bilateral approach fosters balanced brain coordination, crucial for daily tasks like dressing and feeding, outperforming unilateral systems.

Our innovative approach combines rehabilitation science with gamification elements to create an engaging and effective recovery experience for patients, sparking new hope in rehabilitation.

Structural Design

The Armspark structure, designed using Fusion 360, incorporates a lightweight aluminum base, servo-driven shafts, sleeves, gears, and handles, ensuring stability and flexibility. Powered by an Arduino Nano and a 7.4V battery, the system uses a servo driver board for precise control, with a switch for mode selection to enhance reliability.

The equipment's compact design, fitting within a foot-sized base, maximizes portability and practicality. This innovation benefits patients by offering an entertaining, self-managed rehabilitation process, improving motor function and mental well-being.

Design Process

Structurally, we used Fusion 360 to design the Armspark equipment. We employed the following specific strategies: Stretch, cut, trim, circle, square, lock size, spline, straight line, circular array, mirror, vertical, colinear, tangent, concentric, etc. to draw every part of the equipment.

1

Aluminum Plate

This is the main component of the base as well as the arms that extend out.

2

Shaft and Sleeve

These are 3D printed supporting components that provide structural integrity.

3

Gears

These standardize all servo movement so the system moves together - one gear will affect the other.

4

Connecting Rod

Connects the base to the servo gear, enabling precise movement transmission.

5

Handle Design

Designed for easy grip, with 3 servos powering the rotational movement on the wrist.

After the final diagram was complete, we used the laser cutter to cut out the acrylic base plate and connecting rod and we used the 3D printer to print out the shaft, sleeve, and gears. The building process was relatively straightforward though; we start from the base and accumulate and go higher to ensure the stability of the equipment in case of issue with breaking the equipment due to unstable structure.

After testing, we discovered that the acrylic material can't withhold enough weight to carry out the rehabilitation process. This made us change to the aluminum material base plate and connecting rod to make it more stable to withhold the potential weight of an arm. Lastly, we repeat the process to build the second used for the opposite arm and we added the handle to each of them to make the equipment more flexible in terms of the extent of movement.

Testing & Development

Testing revealed challenges such as gear collisions, servo range limitations, and material durability, which were systematically addressed through innovative solutions:

Challenge: Gear Collisions

Initial testing revealed issues with gear collisions during operation, potentially damaging the mechanical components.

Solution: Coordinate Adjustments

Implemented precise coordinate adjustments and optimized gear positioning to eliminate collisions and ensure smooth operation.

Challenge: Servo Range Limitations

Servo motors had limited range of motion, restricting the device's rehabilitation capabilities.

Solution: Joint Limit Functions

Developed and implemented joint limit functions to maximize servo range while preventing damage from over-extension.

Challenge: Material Durability

Original acrylic components couldn't withstand the weight and stress of rehabilitation exercises.

Solution: Material Upgrades

Upgraded to aluminum for base plate and connecting rods, significantly improving durability and weight capacity.

Additional improvements included implementing a debounce function and correcting baud rate to 9600 to ensure smooth servo operation. The Armspark equipment now provides reliable, effective rehabilitation training for hemiplegic patients.
